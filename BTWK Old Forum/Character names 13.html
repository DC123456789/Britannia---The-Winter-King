  7. BlackEagle78 - 23-10-2015, 17:57
    With Germanization, I mean changing the Latinized, clerical spelling of names into more realistic names using a various range of methods:
    • Replacing most ''c''s'' (instead of in the combination ''ch'' in some cultures) with ''k's''.
    • Modifying the names according to (sometimes modern) cultural and regional dialects. An example is the Thuringian name ''Deodwald''. The common way to write it would be ''Theotwald'', but after looking at the Thuringian dialect (which pronounces ''th'' and ''t'' as a softer ''d'', and ''b'' as a ''w''), I decided upon a new spelling. I intend to do this more often with other (mostly German, seeing how their dialects are documented the most) cultures as well.
    • Having a thought and thinking ''Would a 6th Century Thuringian really pronounce it ''Theotbald?'' while keeping the previous reason in my mind. In which other ways have the mainly Latin monastic records possibly corrupted the name?
    I try to portray the names as closely as how they would actually have been pronounced by the people who gave them. I think this is only fair, seeing how, although we are making a mod for a video game, ought to portray history as faithfully as possible, and I don't think it's fair to portray all these peoples' names as just one bland namelist; imagine the possibilities! It feels very different playing as ''Thiudareiks'' than playing as a bland ''Theoderic'', right? Through these names, we can give a little bit more colour to the cultures.
    For the Old Saxon names, I would look at the modern day Saxon dialect, at the few recorded names there are (I already researched a bit and gosh they are scarce) and at the names already in the mod. I would then modify them following the forementioned scheme I mentioned.
    That's basically how I've been doing the name lists untill now. So yeah, I think it's worked so far. I have to puzzle a little bit with every different culture, but it mainly works out.
  8. DC123456789 - 23-10-2015, 19:36
    Keep in mind that modern Saxon dialects are heavily influenced by High German, and that the Saxon language of the time period would have been very close and probably next to identical to the Old English that we all know of the 7th century and later, and probably kind of far away from modern Low German. Old English texts also seem to prefer to use C over K for that sound, so I don't think that it entirely makes sense for the Ingvaeonic cultures to have the 'c's replaced with 'k's.

    And I think I've said it before, but in my opinion that while historical authenticity is good and adds flavour, it still doesn't trump recognizability, especially of famous historical figures. To me, it means a lot more to be playing the famous Theodoric the Great than whoever "Þiudareiks" is.

  10. DC123456789 - 24-10-2015, 12:16
    The Saxons had a significant amount of contact with the Romans even at the very beginning of the mod's timeline, engaging with trade as well as fighting the Roman Empire on many occasions, and of course as they settled the formerly Romano-British areas of Britannia they would have absorbed even more Roman influence; the "barbarian" Germanic tribes are generally considered to have been significantly more Romanized than they are typically given credit for. While the Saxons didn't write anything down per se in Germany, the Old English texts do give us a pretty good idea of how it would have been written down; notably, the letter 'k' was almost never used in Old English orthography as opposed to other Germanic ones where yes, it is usually used in place of c, and the rune "cen" that they used before the Latin alphabet is also usually transliterated as a 'c'. I would argue that using 'c' or 'k' is one of the defining points of Old English orthography, and thus by extension the rest of the Ingnaevones. Also note that Old Dutch (i.e. Frankish), which was quite similar to Old Saxon, didn't like to use the letter 'k' until at least the tenth century, preferring 'c' before that. Besides, there is little to no phonetic difference between using a 'c' versus a 'k' in these contexts, as there wasn't a soft c in any of these languages yet.
