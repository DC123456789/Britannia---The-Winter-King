  2. Frednutts - 21-03-2015, 09:22
    I'm going to add in the various events and history for the two civil wars either today or tomorrow, but I'd like to sort them out first.

    The first is c. 435, and is fought between Aurelius Ambrosius the Elder and High King Vortigern - the question is, why? It's before the adventus, so Saxons don't really play a role. We know that it's essentially a clash between the pro-Celtic and pro-Roman factions, so perhaps we could link it to Aurelius appealing to Aetius for help (The Groans of the Britons), which took place c.445 - Vortigern's victory could then be used as context to invite Saxons to settle, but if Aurelius were victorious (which he shouldn't be most of the time), then he could request some sort of help from Rome - anything from an invasion, to some sort of Roman foederate system.

    The second is c.455, after Vortigern trades Cantia to Hengist and Horsa in return for Rowena - his son Vortimer (who should be held in a positive light as opposed to Vortigern) usurps the High Kingship from him with basically the support of everyone, and Vortigern flees to Kent. The first battle at Derguentid is a defeat for the Britons, and after that Vortimer is killed by Rowena, which should end the war in a white peace and return the High Kingship to Vortigern (possibly by event). War should break out again, this time led by another of Vortigern's sons, Cadeyrn (Gorfyddyd's gradnfather), who then fights at Rithergabail, which is a minor victory for the Britons - though Cadeyrn and Horsa are both slain. This should probably also end in a white peace (by event, probably), which would then trigger the Treachery of the Long Knives, which, as I mentioned previously, should be a whole event chain in itself.

  4. Frednutts - 21-03-2015, 17:22
    True - perhaps a victory for Ambrosius would be more applicable.

    That page you linked is originally from EBK, which also agrees that AAtE and AA are two different people - they both however agree that Wallop was fought under AAtE, around 437 - if it were around the Rowena debacle (c.455), then AAtE would be dead, and AA would only be a youth, so it doesn't really fit. The line about him being granted 'all the kingdoms of the western side of Britain' I've taken to understand as meaning he was given responsibility for their restructuring - including the invitation of Cunedda Wledig to become Governor of North Wales - rather than actually being awarded them in person. Vortigern and AAtE are basically the two most senior persons in Britain at the time - both essentially seeing themselves and governors of the entire island in a Roman manner, but with the former's de facto control lying in the west, and the latter's in the south - this conflict of position would be enough for the reasons behind Wallop, which would also make the victory condition of granting AAtE control of the western kingdoms make more sense, as it would suggest that in his defeat, Vortigern recognises AAtE's seniority as rightful ruler of the Britons.

    On the topic of Romans, and what they're up to at the time, if anything, c.450 is probably the most likely time for a Roman reinvasion of Britain - after the Battle of the Catalaunian Plains (451), Roman control in Gaul is as high as it will be before the empire's collapse, and if Aetius hadn't been assassinated in 454, a reinvasion of Britannia would not have been out of the picture, especially in the brief period of time between the Catalaunian Plains and Hengist's revolt (451 - 455). After Aetius' death in 454 though, Roman rule in Gaul never really recovers, and any attempts at retrieving Britain for the empire are snuffed out.
  5. DC123456789 - 21-03-2015, 18:39
    The problem with the dating is that there's a conflict between Nennius's dates and Bede's. The dates of 437 for Guoloph and 442 for AAtE dying come from interpretations of Nennius, who puts the Adventus in 428/9. The late 440s and 450s date for the Adventus and the Rowena debacle comes from Bede and the Anglo-Saxon chronicle, who basically move everything about that period up about 15 years or so. This makes it kind of awkward when you try to pull dates from both chronologies, because then the events don't really line up properly. For example, how would AAtE die of disease in the midst of the chaos of the Saxon rebellion of the Adventus hasn't even happened yet? It would probably be better just to choose one chronology and stick with it; for example, if we're making 449 the Adventus then it would make the most sense for Guoloph to happen in 458, as some have suggested it did.

  7. Frednutts - 21-03-2015, 20:12
    Fair enough, I take your point - we'll have the civil wars all after the adventus then, c.458; Guoloph, Derguentid, and Rithergabail.

    Here's a rough (dateless) chronology:

    - The so called 'Rowena Debacle'; Hengist becomes King of Cantia, Vortigern now universally hated by the Britons.
    - Council of Romano-Britons under Ambrosius Aurelianus, who refuses to recognise Vortigern, elect to usurp him. Civil war begins with Aurelianus on one side, and Vortigern and Hengist on the other.
    - Battle of Guoloph; Aurelianus is victorious, Vortigern is defeated, but not severely. Aurelianus granted rule of Glouvia.
    - Civil war breaks out again soon after when Aurelianus' faction elects Vortimer has High King, usurping Vortigern. Civil war begins with Aurelianus and Vortimer on one side, and Vortigern and Hengist on the other.
    - Battle of Derguentid; Vortimer defeated, and is poisoned by Rowena soon after. Vortigern victorious, and reclaims the High Kingship, but is badly weakened by his universal lack of support from the Britons.
    - Pro-Roman faction nominates another usurper, another of Vortigern's sons, Cadeyrn. Civil war breaks out with Aurelianus and Cadeyrn on one side, and Vortimer and Hengist on the other.
    - Battle of Rithergabail; pyrrhic victory for the Britons. Cadeyrn is however slain, as is Horsa. Vortigern retains the High Kingship.
    - In the face of more civil wars, the Sais under Hengist in Cantia arrange a peace conference. The Treachery of the Long Knives commences, wherein large amounts of Romano-British nobility is killed. Vortigern is ransomed in return for Saxon control over all of Sussex, Essex, and Middlesex. Vortigern retreats to his stronghold, and is killed by Aurelianus soon after.

    And yes, I was planning on giving Aurelius the Lundein title, and all it's de jure territory - it makes sense.
