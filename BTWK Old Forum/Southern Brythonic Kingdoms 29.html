    I think we decided that Rhegin, Celemion and Gwintguic are all part of a de jure kingdom with Belgic roots. Ceint and Lundein should be another de jure kingdom, and Went and Colun should be de jure Icenia as they already area. I can easily retrieve old data so it doesn't particularly matter. Pelagianism has spread too far - DC seemed to strike a healthy balance with them earlier and their presence in Avalon in 479 doesn't make sense. The original de jure Ceint was just Luca responding to my requests for Briton de jure reclaim, so he didn't bother to research it much. Rhegin seems to be divided from Ceint by being less urbanised (and Romanised) and having more in common with the less civilised Britons. Ceint was probably fairly powerful and likely had close links to magistrates in Londinium itself.

    I'm going to draft a Briton de jure duchy and kingdom map, I'd like all of us to agree on its final form so we have a stylistic manual to work from in future if we're filling in data from 410-479.

    In effect, there are four kingdoms of note:
    1) The Belgic Kingdom (provisional name) encompassing Celemion, the Belgae and Rhegin. Most probably we need one more de jure duchy formed from parts of Cadarn and Celemion to do it justice.
    2) Cynwidion, made up of Lerion, Chiltern, Stronggore and Calchwynedd.

    I do think we can better represent the failing Roman administration in central southern Britain though - this map (which we've all seen a hundred times) better represents Britannia before Vortigern's High Kingship, while the other one we're familiar with is better for after. Though centralised Roman administration had technically left Britannia, the Romano-Britons basically continued to act as if nothing had changed - Ceint only emerged as an independent kingdom c.409, but most of the rest of central southern Britain remained banded together in some sort of pseudo-Roman style for at most another decade. Plus, having a Roman provincular title there would really show the state of post-Roman decay the country is in - the title could be set to collapse by event, and the kingdoms would emerge from underneath it.
    It's just that the style of Celtic kingdoms we see in TWK just didn't exist in 410 - the rulers didn't think of themselves as kings, just administrators of regions within a larger, Roman-esque entity.
    Just some thoughts...
