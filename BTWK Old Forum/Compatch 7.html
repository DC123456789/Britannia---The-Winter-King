  1. Ols - 31-10-2014, 20:45
    It might have been, but the books are pretty explicit that the Britons and Romano-Britons are using some form of single heir succession law. There's no mention of Powys splitting on Gorfydyyd's death - which is the main death of a King with more than one potential heir in the books. Siluria is indeed split in two on Gundleus' death, but between two foreign conquerors - Lancelot and Oengus Mac Airem. Gwent does not split on Tewdric's abdication, and instead remains a fairly stable semi-Roman realm. The Cumbrians can and should be different - but there does need to be some way for strong rulers to keep things together more.

    I think what we should focus on is allowing the split to happen through our own control - perhaps if Powys falls into anarchy then Pengwern should just become indepenent, and the King of Powys should gain the duchy title of his capital and the appropriate vassals to match. The same goes for other places. It'd be pretty annoying if it hits anyone playing a game if their primary heir suddenly becomes unlanded and their primary title dissolves. Some titles could start with authority that slowly falls and drags them into Gavelkind, rather than beginning with it. The general theme does seem to be that things are getting worse, whereas the theme of the base Crusader Kings is that things slowly get better tech and laws wise.

    The really frustrating thing that I hope to correct another time is that there still aren't strong enough systems to break up Anglo-Saxon titles in a way that is acceptable to both a player in the area and to someone facing the AI. I'd prefer to see something reasonably similar to the Heptarchy form most of the time - assuming the Britons do lose ground at all. In the future I'm going to build a Heptarchy bookmark to serve as some kind of proving ground for tech that is directed mostly at keeping Saxon realms diverse and varied.
