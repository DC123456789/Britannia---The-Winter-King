  5. BlackEagle78 - 22-02-2016, 17:30
    Eotaland could be changed back if it is too localised, I have no qualms about that. For the -lands: I had the mindset that the titles represent the land itself and not the tribes who once lived there. I don't think that Norsemen later in the timeframe identified as ''Secge'' or ''Teuton'', or even ''Mofding'' (an Angle (and possible even Anglo-Saxon) term (!)). I see these areas as bearing these names because of old legends and stories, but mostly tradition, and not a feeling of connection to these old, vague tribes. If these were cultures on their own, I would understand naming them just that, but because they're provinces (and they're going to have the same name until the end of the timeframe), I decided for this approach (and after all, aren't ''Vestrgautland'' and ''Austrgautland'' also things?).

    Could you please show me the source with gives the region that name? I want to be able to use it as an inspiration when giving it e.g. Roman or Frankish names as well (as I've been doing with the rest of Scandinavia for now).

    Also, would anybody be so kind as to make the island of Als in Denmark and the area of Riseby south of the Schlei (the region of Schwansen) their own provinces? I feel like that would represent internal borders better. Schwansen would have only one holding and Als could have two (based on the amount of settlements in those regions). Thank you a great lot. Finding and appropriating settlement and province names for Denmark is a huge pain and costs a lot of time. I would be very thankful for your help.
  6. DC123456789 - 22-02-2016, 17:42
    I meant it really only for the Jutish/Angle/Anglo-Saxon names, since in that case they really do represent the tribes themselves (especially the Mofdings and Myrgings); the current Norse names are fine, even preferable.

    The Sae-Dene (the Anglo-Saxon name, obviously; a Norse version would be better) are mentioned in the Widsith poem; I've also seen them referred to as Hocings in other sources, after their (presumed) founder. They're not tied to any region specifically, but given that they're supposed to be interacting with the Frisians (as in Finnesburg) and seem to have been founded before the final "fall" of the Jutes, it seemed like the most appropriate place. The Sycgs and Gordum are similar in that they're named as Norse kingdoms in Jutland, but there are no specifics about where they actually were and are therefore somewhat arbitrarily identified with one of the known chiefdom/kingdom areas.

  9. BlackEagle78 - 22-02-2016, 19:06
    Can you give me a source where Gorđum is named? It would be handier for coming up with names for other cultures as well. One for the Sae-Denes would be handy as well.

    Again, the -ing suffix indicates a descend from a common founder (such as Myrg for the Myrgings, Scyld for the Scyldings, etc.). This makes sense for a tribe, which exists and is bound by a common extended clan (or at least such a clan as leadership of the tribe). By adding -land you're indicating that it isn't the clan or tribe itself which you're ruling, but the land on which that mythical tribe once lived. And that's how it is; if the ruler of these duchies decides to become feudal or urban or whatever we decide to name it, the idea of tribe becomes more and more vague over time; thus, a feudal lord would see the land as a region named after an old tribe, but not as the direct homeland of that tribe as if it were ruled by said tribe at that moment.
