  1. JasperClay - 01-03-2015, 14:55
    1.) Okay, so I'd like to propose the following as a balancing mechanism. It's not perfectly elegant, but it would essentially combine Ols' "pressure" idea with the normal building tree.

    Buildings in CK2 represent infrastructure, as they should. Of course, no mechanic in the game represents population - with the possible exception of holdings, but they're also really infrastructure. In most games and most mods, infrastructure works as a decent proxy for population, but it doesn't work at all in the "Dark Ages". The problem isn't a lack of urban structure: it's that there aren't enough people in the city to make the network effect of proximity worthwhile.

    So, let's make a building that represents population.

    I'm thinking of something like a 7-rank tree.
    i.) The lowest rank is "Desolate" - it gives -90% levies and -90% taxes. Maybe negative 95%. Desolate makes the province nearly immune to disease, and over an order of magnitude easier to convert, but is obviously undesirable.
    ii.)Next is "Depopulated," which is -50%/-50%. This should grant a hefty bonus to disease resistance, and make a province 5x more likely to convert.
    iii.)"Underpopulated" would be -25%/-25%. It should be easier to convert this province, and it should have a minimal bonus to disease resistance.
    iv.) "Normal Population" has no effects.
    v.)"Densely Populated" could give something like +20% levies and +15% taxes, but decrease disease resistance. I'd leave it neutral on conversion, or barely harder.
    vi.) "Overpopulated" should give +30% levies and 20% taxes, but decrease disease resistance and conversion chance significantly, while increasing revolt risk.
    vii.) "Teeming" should give 35% levies and 20% taxes, but cripple disease resistance and conversion chance, while significantly increasing revolt risk.

    In 410, much of Britain should be "Underpopulated" and "Depopulated," due to the massive troop loads taken off to the empire, never to return, in the 4th century. I'd argue for a similar situation in 479, but less evenly distributed. So, perhaps Glevum is "Underpopulated," and Corinium and Exter are "Depopulated," but Durnovaria and Lindinis and Caer Swys are "Normal". London, on the other hand, is "Desolate". This would also make my "city" buildings work as intended. So, York and Corinium and Lincoln are all 5's, but varyingly underpopulated. Glevum's only a 4, but more populated than Corinium, in 479. London's a 6, but deserted, etc.

    Population should be an exceptionally cheap building to create. Like, 50 gold, maybe. However, it should take 20-25 years to build normally. Repopulating an area is a laborious process, and even sacking a city in a raid should have a chance to reduce population. "Densely Populated" should be the highest level one is allowed to build, and should be location-locked to areas that could support this. (Historical major cities, river estuaries, etc.) Overcrowded and teeming would only happen by event.

    Disease, like the plague of Justinian, should have event-related chances to reduce population. Capturing a different-culture province should be likely to reduce the population in that province by 1, and should possibly reduce it by 2 in, say, 1 of 4 holdings. However, at approximately 30%-50% of this rate, populations should relocate when their city is captured. So, in this way, the Saxons will have to contend with the British holdings they capture becoming weaker, while remaining British holdings actually get stronger from the emigration.

    Saxon holdings themselves could start out at a population level that allows for balance with the British. The "boats of Saxons arriving" events could increase population even as they add event troops, and be one of the main ways "Teeming" or "Overcrowded" holdings actually occur. When the owner of a "Teeming" or "Overcrowded" holding ends a war, their teeming or overcrowded holdings should shrink. If they lost the war, this should refill their levies. If they won the war, this should repopulate the newly taken territory. This should happen to a much lesser extent with "Densely" populated holdings - maybe never. Likewise, Pictish rulers could be given "Teeming" and "Overcrowded" provinces before their major invasions.

    (…continued)

    2.) If this idea is a good one, then I'd also suggest the following. Saxon-British wars should be fought for duchies more often, but have longer truces when they are. That would let this mechanic play into those already-scripted invasions better.

    3.) Furthermore, if we like this plan, then I'll overhaul the whole building tree as a part of it. About a year ago, I half-coded a modmodmod for aGoT that was variously called "Return on Investment," "Net-Present Value," or "Not a Building Overhaul". Essentially, the idea was to make spending your money on holdings, buildings, and infrastructure a better idea, long-term, than hoarding in for mercenaries. In general, holdings got much cheaper to build, but took massively longer. Economic buildings began to take over a decade to make, but had a barely noticeable start-up cost. It changed the question from "Should I build this?" to "Should I build this now, or do I need levy-buildings, first?". Therefore, the price of levy-generating buildings was normalized to be better than mercenaries. I'd still need to do the math for retinues, but it shouldn't be too hard.

    4.) Retinues might actually grow in importance as a result of this. I'd ask if I could reduce the retinue cap added per holding, and attach more weight to buildings when calculating retinues. Therefore, retinue count would be more about demesne infrastructure than about realm size. This will make vassals more powerful vis-a-vis their liege's, and reduce the human-steamroller win-button of retinues + vassal mercs. Retinues won't really expand as you conquer, just as you increase your ability to house and pay your men. Mercenaries should be a real luxury good, unless they're vassalized (and vassal mercs need to be reduced in size, dramatically.) So I won't be able to wipe the Wessex invasion with retinues, Magnis Fort demesne troops, and the Brotherhood of Britain + Wolftails.


    -Summation-
    Basically, I want players to have to think like Arthur, when they play TWK. It's not just about winning the war and taking the territory, rinse and repeat. It's about restoring something that once was, because if you don't, your victories will be pyrrhic. The enemy can grow stronger even as they're squeezed in, and you can be crippled even if you win, if the plague hits after you've let your cities be sieged. I don't think this will solve all balancing problems, but I think overhauling buildings could delivery a very elegant user-end balancing experience. I realize it's not easy to code, but it allows us to use fun mechanics to balance the map, rather than sledgehammer scripted events at players.

    There's no way I'll have the time or experience to do all of this, especially the event-coding, myself. So it won't happen unless others on the team like it enough and find it important enough to work on, themselves. My apologies, but I just picked up two more part-time jobs: I'm planning on buying a PC laptop today, which will allow to work in-transit, but I won't gain any productivity, since I'm going to be SAT tutoring.

  7. JasperClay - 01-03-2015, 16:30
    1.) So, I'm thinking of the 3rd century as "Normal" Population. I figure that probably compares decently to the early-Viking age population. This way, "densely populated" would still be special in 700-800AD. I feel like by the beginning of the 5th century, we've already seen a fall from 2nd-3rd century populations. The question is really whether the 2nd-3rd century was "Dense" or "Normal," especially as compared to the 8th-9th centuries. I don't have answers here, so I'll defer to whatever is easy.

    2.) Just the emigration events. Like, when the plagues hits and reduces populations across the board, that shouldn't be too bad. But when the Saxons take Calleva, and the population drops from, say, "Normal" to "Depopulated," how hard is it to write an event that increases the population modifier in Corinium or Durnovaria by 1? Also, I wouldn't want them to move to Venta - first because it's described as a "poor frontier town," in the books, and second because I think the refugees would move to a non-adjacent duchy. I just don't know what this would entail.

    3.) Preview: aGoT NPV calculations:
    i.) Cities:
    Old:
    2yrs at 0 (revenue derived while taking 2 years to build)
    12 per year, after
    500 base cost.
    43 ⅔ years Return on Investment (if you capture all the revenue, this is how long it takes to break even. Really, more like 100-150, depending on tax.)

    New:
    20 years at 0 (revenue while taking 20 years to build and populate the city).
    12 per year, after
    150 base cost
    32.5 year ROI (if you capture all the revenue. Really more like 70-100, depending on tax.)
    Building a City creates a 5% levy malus in the county (populating the city). Ideally, maybe a -1% reduction for all the builder's holdings, so it hurts large, decentralized realms more.

    ii.) Castle:
    Old:
    2 years at 0
    4 per year, after
    500 base cost.
    127 year ROI (more if not directly held)

    New:
    10 years at 0
    4 per year, after
    150 base cost
    47.5 year ROI (more if not directly held)
    Building a Castle creates a 5% tax malus in the county (or 1% over all the builder's holdings.)
    #The total cost should = ~the cost of employing as many soldiers as provided by the castle for ten-fifteen years).

    iii.) Temple:
    Old:
    2 years at 0
    8 per year
    500 base cost
    64.5 year ROI (again, this is closer to 150-200 years, since you won't hold it directly.)

    New:
    15 years at 0
    8 per year, after
    33.75 ROI
    -2% levy, -3% tax while building. (or .5 and .5 over all the builder's holdings.)

    I know the base costs are different in TWK, but I shoot for an ~40-50 year ROI on castles, and a 25-35 year ROI on temples/cities. For economic buildings, the ROI is usually 20-30 years. For combined econ+levy buildings, the ROI is more like 50 years.

    4.) I'm totally cool with that. Then let me amend the proposal to this:
    i.) Make the 'decentralization' modifiers affect retinues.
    ii.) Cut levies by, say, 50%. Cut retinues by only 25%. (Or even leave them as is, and just make decentralization more logarithmic overall.)
    iii.) Make military buildings add much fewer levies, and more retinues.
    iv.) Cut the size of retinue subunits to 50-100, so we get more book-size war bands.
